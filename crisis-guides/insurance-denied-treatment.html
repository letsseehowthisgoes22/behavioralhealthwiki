My Insurance Just Denied Treatment: Emergency Steps to Take

Immediate Action Steps (First 15 Minutes)

Getting an insurance denial when your child needs treatment feels devastating. Your panic is normal and understandable. But right now, you need to act fast and smart. These first steps can make the difference between getting help today versus waiting weeks.

Step 1: Document everything immediately. Write down the date, time, and who told you about the denial. If you got the news by phone, ask for the representative's name and ID number. Take a photo or screenshot of any denial letter or online message. This documentation becomes evidence in your appeal.[1]

Step 2: Ask for the specific denial reason. Insurance companies must give you the exact medical reason they denied care. Common reasons include "not medically necessary" or "services available at lower level of care." Get this in writing. If you're on the phone, say: "I need the specific clinical criteria your company used to deny this treatment. Please email this to me within the hour."

Step 3: Request an urgent peer-to-peer review. Ask to speak to a supervisor immediately. Say: "My child needs urgent treatment and I'm requesting an immediate peer-to-peer review with your medical director. This cannot wait for standard processing." Most insurance companies have expedited review processes for urgent situations that must be completed within 72 hours.[2]

Step 4: Call the treatment facility now. Contact the program that was denied and explain the situation. Many facilities have insurance specialists who can help file urgent appeals or suggest alternative options. Ask: "What can we do right now while we fight this denial? Do you have any emergency beds or payment plans?"

Is This a Safety Emergency?

Before spending time on insurance battles, you must assess whether your child is in immediate danger. Some situations require emergency medical care regardless of insurance approval.

Call 911 immediately if your child has: Active suicidal thoughts with a plan, threats to harm others, psychosis (seeing or hearing things that aren't there), signs of overdose, or severe self-harm behavior. Tell the operator: "My teenager is having a psychiatric emergency and needs immediate evaluation."[3]

Go to the emergency room if your child shows: Complete refusal to eat or drink for 24+ hours, manic behavior that's escalating (no sleep for days, increasingly risky behavior), or withdrawal symptoms from alcohol or drugs that could be dangerous. Emergency rooms cannot legally turn away patients having psychiatric crises, even without insurance pre-authorization.

Contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline if you're unsure about safety levels. Call 988 or chat online at 988lifeline.org. Crisis counselors can help you assess whether your situation requires immediate emergency response or can wait for insurance resolution. They can also connect you with local mobile crisis teams.

Remember: Emergency psychiatric holds for safety reasons don't require insurance pre-approval. The insurance battle comes later. Your child's immediate safety is the priority. Many families worry about costs, but emergency psychiatric care is often covered under different insurance rules than planned treatment admissions.

Critical Actions in the First 24 Hours

Once you've handled any immediate safety concerns, you have a narrow window to turn around an insurance denial. Insurance companies count on families giving up after the first "no." Your persistence in the next 24 hours often determines success.

File a formal appeal immediately. Don't wait for denial letters to arrive by mail. Most insurance companies accept appeals by phone, fax, or online portal. Say: "I'm filing a formal appeal for [your child's name], member ID [number]. This is an urgent appeal for mental health treatment that was denied today." Ask for the appeal confirmation number and the name of who took your call.[4]

Contact your child's doctor or therapist. The treatment provider who recommended the denied care needs to advocate for your child. Ask them to call the insurance company's medical director directly. Peer-to-peer reviews between doctors often succeed where parent appeals fail. Give your provider all the denial information and ask them to document their call.

Reach out to your employer's HR department. If you have insurance through work, HR departments often have direct contacts at insurance companies. They can escalate your case quickly because they represent hundreds of employees. Call HR and say: "My child was denied urgent mental health treatment and I need help escalating this with [insurance company name]. This is affecting my ability to work."

Research alternative coverage options. While fighting the denial, look into backup plans. Check if your child qualifies for emergency Medicaid coverage in your state. Some states provide immediate temporary coverage for psychiatric emergencies. Contact your state's insurance commissioner's office for emergency assistance programs. Many states have special funds for mental health crises that insurance won't cover.

What NOT to Do

Panic leads to mistakes that can hurt your case. These common errors can delay care or weaken your appeal, even when your child desperately needs help.

Don't threaten legal action immediately. Saying "I'll sue you" usually makes insurance representatives less helpful and can slow down your case. Focus on medical necessity and urgency instead. You can always pursue legal options later, but threats often backfire in the crisis moment.

Don't accept the first denial without fighting. Insurance companies deny about 17% of mental health claims initially, but approve many of these same claims on appeal.[5] The first denial is often automatic. Your real chance comes with the appeal process, especially if you can get doctors involved.

Don't wait for regular business hours. Many insurance companies have 24-hour nurse lines and urgent appeal processes. Don't assume you have to wait until Monday if the denial comes on Friday. Mental health crises don't follow business schedules, and your insurance shouldn't either.

Don't discuss financial hardship as your main argument. While costs matter to families, insurance appeals focus on medical necessity. Lead with your child's clinical condition and why they need this specific level of care. Financial concerns are secondary in the appeal process.

Warning Signs Your Child's Condition Is Worsening

While fighting insurance battles, you must monitor your child's mental state closely. Denial of treatment can cause rapid deterioration, especially in conditions like bipolar disorder or severe anxiety. Watch for these escalation signs that require immediate action.

Immediate emergency signs: New or increasing talk about suicide, giving away belongings, sudden mood improvement after severe depression (can indicate suicide planning), complete loss of touch with reality, or dangerous impulsive behavior. If you see these signs, call 911 or go to the emergency room immediately. Don't wait for insurance approval.

Urgent concern signs: Rapid mood swings getting more extreme, sleep patterns completely disrupted (staying awake for days or sleeping 18+ hours), refusing all food or water, aggressive behavior toward family members, or signs of drug or alcohol use as self-medication. These warrant crisis line calls and possible emergency room visits.

Increasing risk signs: Social isolation becoming more complete, school refusal or work absence, hygiene deteriorating rapidly, increased anxiety or panic attacks, or hopeless statements about the future. These signs mean your insurance appeal is becoming more urgent and you may need to consider out-of-pocket emergency options.

Document any worsening symptoms with dates and times. This information strengthens your insurance appeal and helps emergency room doctors if you need crisis intervention. Many families keep a daily log during insurance battles to track their child's condition objectively.

Week One: Building Your Appeal Strategy

The first week after an insurance denial determines whether you'll get coverage or face months of delays. This is when you build a case that's hard for insurance companies to ignore. Success requires organization, persistence, and strategic thinking.

Gather supporting documentation. Collect all medical records showing your child's diagnosis and previous treatments. Include hospitalization records, therapy notes, medication trials, and school documentation of problems. Insurance appeals succeed when you prove medical necessity with comprehensive evidence. Contact every provider who has treated your child and request records immediately.[6]

Understand your insurance plan's appeal timeline. Most plans allow 30 days for standard appeals, but urgent appeals must be decided within 72 hours. If your child's condition is worsening, always request expedited review. File a second-level appeal immediately if the first is denied. Many insurance approvals come at the second appeal level when more senior medical reviewers get involved.

Consider getting an independent medical evaluation. If your child has co-occurring conditions or complex symptoms, an independent psychiatrist's evaluation can strengthen your case. This costs money upfront but often leads to insurance approval that saves thousands long-term. Some states require insurance to pay for independent evaluations when appeals are filed.

Contact your state insurance commissioner. Every state has an insurance department that handles consumer complaints. File a complaint online or by phone, explaining that your child was denied urgent mental health treatment. State insurance departments can pressure companies to resolve cases quickly and sometimes override denials entirely.

Research treatment alternatives while appealing. Look into intensive outpatient programs, partial hospitalization, or crisis stabilization services that might be approved more easily. Sometimes getting your child into a lower level of care quickly leads to step-up approval when they prove they need more intensive treatment. Work with providers to develop backup treatment plans.

Emergency Resources and Crisis Contacts

Having immediate access to crisis resources can save your child's life when insurance denials delay treatment. Keep these numbers easily accessible and don't hesitate to use them. Crisis services are designed to bridge gaps when regular treatment isn't available.

National Crisis Lines: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline provides 24/7 crisis counseling and local resource connections. Call 988 or chat at 988lifeline.org. Crisis Text Line offers immediate support via text - send HOME to 741741. Both services are free and available regardless of insurance status.[7]

SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 provides 24/7 treatment referrals and information services. They can help you find emergency treatment options in your area, including facilities that offer sliding scale fees or emergency funding. They also provide information about state emergency mental health programs.

Local Mobile Crisis Teams: Most communities have mobile crisis response teams that come to your home during psychiatric emergencies. These teams can assess your child's safety, provide immediate stabilization, and help connect to emergency services. Call your local emergency dispatch (911) and ask for mobile crisis response, or contact your county mental health department.

Emergency Room Protocols: When taking your child to the emergency room for psychiatric crisis, bring all medication bottles, insurance cards, and a list of current providers. Tell triage staff immediately: "My child is having a psychiatric emergency and needs mental health evaluation." Emergency rooms must provide medical screening regardless of insurance pre-authorization status.

Clinical Significance: Insurance denials during mental health crises create dangerous delays in care that can worsen outcomes and increase suicide risk. Immediate advocacy and crisis resource utilization can bridge treatment gaps while appeals are processed. Research shows that rapid response to insurance barriers significantly improves treatment access and reduces emergency interventions.
