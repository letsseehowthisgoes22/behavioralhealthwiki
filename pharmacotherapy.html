Pharmacotherapy for Substance Use Disorders

Overview

Medication-assisted treatment (MAT) — more recently referred to as medications for addiction treatment (MOUD) in the context of opioid use disorder — represents one of the most significant advances in addiction medicine. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration has approved medications for the treatment of alcohol, opioid, and nicotine use disorders, with evidence demonstrating that these medications, when combined with psychosocial interventions, produce superior outcomes to either approach alone.[1]

Despite the strength of the evidence, pharmacotherapy remains underutilized in addiction treatment. SAMHSA data indicate that fewer than 35% of specialty addiction treatment programs offer any FDA-approved medication for substance use disorders.[1] This underutilization reflects a combination of philosophical resistance (particularly from programs rooted in the twelve-step tradition, which historically emphasized abstinence from all mood-altering substances), regulatory barriers (particularly for buprenorphine prescribing), and insufficient training of treatment program staff in pharmacological management.

Alcohol Use Disorder

Three medications have FDA approval for the treatment of alcohol use disorder: naltrexone, acamprosate, and disulfiram. Each targets a different neurobiological mechanism and is appropriate for different clinical presentations.

Naltrexone

Naltrexone is an opioid receptor antagonist that reduces the rewarding effects of alcohol by blocking opioid-mediated dopamine release in the mesolimbic system. Available in both oral (50mg daily) and extended-release injectable (380mg monthly, marketed as Vivitrol) formulations, naltrexone has demonstrated efficacy in reducing heavy drinking days and, in some studies, promoting abstinence. The COMBINE study, published in JAMA in 2006, found that naltrexone combined with medical management produced outcomes equivalent to a more intensive behavioral intervention — suggesting that medication with relatively brief clinical support can be an effective treatment model.[2]

The injectable formulation (Vivitrol) eliminates the adherence challenges associated with daily oral dosing — a significant advantage in a population where medication non-compliance is common. The primary contraindication is concurrent opioid use or the need for opioid analgesia; naltrexone will precipitate withdrawal in opioid-dependent individuals and will block the analgesic effects of opioid medications.

Acamprosate

Acamprosate (marketed as Campral) is believed to restore the balance between excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmission that is disrupted by chronic alcohol use, primarily through modulation of the glutamatergic system. Acamprosate is most effective in patients who have already achieved initial abstinence — it supports the maintenance of abstinence rather than facilitating the reduction of active drinking. European clinical trials have demonstrated consistent efficacy for acamprosate, though US trials have produced more mixed results — a discrepancy that may reflect differences in study populations and treatment contexts.[3]

Disulfiram

Disulfiram (Antabuse) operates through an entirely different mechanism: it inhibits the enzyme aldehyde dehydrogenase, causing the accumulation of acetaldehyde when alcohol is consumed, producing an intensely unpleasant reaction (flushing, nausea, vomiting, hypotension). Disulfiram's efficacy depends entirely on adherence — when taken as prescribed, it creates a powerful deterrent to drinking. Supervised administration (by a spouse, clinician, or other reliable third party) significantly improves outcomes. While not appropriate for all patients, disulfiram remains a useful tool for motivated patients who benefit from an external deterrent during early recovery.[2]

Opioid Use Disorder

The pharmacotherapy of opioid use disorder (OUD) has the strongest evidence base in addiction medicine. Three medications have FDA approval: methadone, buprenorphine, and naltrexone. These medications reduce opioid use, decrease overdose mortality, reduce criminal activity, and improve social functioning — outcomes that are supported by decades of research and multiple systematic reviews.[4]

Methadone

Methadone, a full mu-opioid receptor agonist with a long half-life (24-36 hours), has been the cornerstone of opioid use disorder treatment since its development for this purpose in the 1960s by Dole and Nyswander. Methadone maintenance treatment reduces illicit opioid use, reduces overdose risk, reduces HIV transmission, and improves social functioning. The Cochrane Review of methadone maintenance treatment (Mattick et al., 2009) found that methadone at adequate doses (60mg or more daily) was associated with statistically significant reductions in heroin use compared to non-pharmacological treatments.[4]

In the United States, methadone for OUD can only be dispensed through federally certified opioid treatment programs (OTPs) — a regulatory restriction that limits access but ensures clinical oversight. Patients typically attend the OTP daily for observed dosing, with increasing take-home privileges as they demonstrate clinical stability.

Buprenorphine

Buprenorphine, a partial mu-opioid agonist, was approved for the treatment of OUD in 2002 and represented a paradigm shift: for the first time, an effective medication for opioid addiction could be prescribed in an office-based setting rather than exclusively through specialized treatment programs. Buprenorphine's partial agonist pharmacology provides several clinical advantages over methadone: a ceiling effect on respiratory depression (improving safety in overdose), less sedation, and a milder withdrawal syndrome upon discontinuation.[5]

The most commonly prescribed formulation is the buprenorphine/naloxone combination (marketed as Suboxone), in which naloxone is included to deter misuse by injection (naloxone is poorly absorbed sublingually but produces immediate withdrawal if the formulation is injected). Extended-release formulations (Sublocade, a monthly injectable) address adherence challenges and reduce diversion risk.

Extended-Release Naltrexone

Extended-release naltrexone (Vivitrol), the same formulation used for alcohol use disorder, has also demonstrated efficacy for OUD. As an opioid antagonist, it blocks the euphoric effects of opioids without producing dependence or withdrawal. The primary challenge is induction — patients must be fully detoxified from opioids (typically 7-10 days) before receiving naltrexone, a requirement that limits its applicability for patients in active use. The X:BOT trial (Lee et al., New England Journal of Medicine, 2018) found that extended-release naltrexone, once successfully initiated, was as effective as buprenorphine-naloxone in preventing relapse — but that the difficulty of initiating naltrexone (requiring opioid-free induction) resulted in more early dropouts.[5]

Nicotine Use Disorder

Tobacco use disorder, while often addressed separately from other substance use disorders in clinical practice, shares the same neurobiological mechanisms and benefits from pharmacological intervention. FDA-approved pharmacotherapies include nicotine replacement therapies (patch, gum, lozenge, inhaler, nasal spray), bupropion (Zyban/Wellbutrin), and varenicline (Chantix). Varenicline, a partial agonist at the alpha-4-beta-2 nicotinic acetylcholine receptor, has the strongest efficacy data — a Cochrane Review found that varenicline approximately triples the odds of long-term abstinence compared to placebo.[6]

Stimulant Use Disorders

No FDA-approved pharmacotherapy currently exists for cocaine or methamphetamine use disorders — a significant gap in the treatment armamentarium given the severity and prevalence of these conditions. Multiple pharmacological agents have been investigated in clinical trials, including topiramate, modafinil, disulfiram, and N-acetylcysteine, with modest or inconsistent results. Contingency management remains the psychosocial intervention with the strongest evidence for stimulant use disorders, and current clinical practice guidelines recommend psychosocial intervention as the primary treatment approach.[1]

Pharmacogenomics

An emerging area of research in addiction pharmacotherapy is pharmacogenomics — the study of how genetic variation influences an individual's response to medications. Genetic variation in the OPRM1 gene (encoding the mu-opioid receptor) has been associated with differential response to naltrexone for alcohol use disorder; variation in dopamine receptor genes may influence response to other pharmacological agents. While pharmacogenomic-guided treatment is not yet standard practice, several premium treatment programs — particularly Swiss programs such as Paracelsus Recovery and The Kusnacht Practice — incorporate extensive genetic and biochemical testing as part of their assessment and treatment planning processes.[7]

Clinical Integration: The evidence strongly supports combining pharmacotherapy with psychosocial intervention for optimal outcomes. Medications address the neurobiological substrates of addiction (craving, withdrawal, reward dysregulation) while psychosocial interventions address the cognitive, behavioral, and social dimensions of the disorder. Treatment programs that do not offer or facilitate access to FDA-approved medications are withholding an evidence-based treatment component that could significantly improve patient outcomes.
