Understanding Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) for Treatment

What Is an Explanation of Benefits

An Explanation of Benefits (EOB) is a detailed document your insurance company sends after you receive medical care. It shows how your insurer processed your claim and what you owe.[1] The EOB is not a bill. Instead, it helps you track your coverage and spot errors.

For behavioral health treatment, EOBs become extra important. Mental health and substance use services often face unique coverage challenges. Your EOB helps you understand if your insurer followed parity laws. These laws require equal coverage for mental health and medical care.[2]

You typically receive an EOB within 30 days of your treatment visit. Some insurers send paper copies by mail. Others provide electronic versions through patient portals. Keep every EOB you receive. You may need them for tax purposes, appeals, or tracking your annual deductible.

The EOB serves multiple purposes beyond just showing costs. It creates a paper trail of your treatment. This documentation becomes vital if you need to appeal a denial or file a complaint about coverage violations. Understanding your EOB gives you power to advocate for proper coverage.

Reading Your EOB: Key Sections

Every EOB contains standard sections with specific information. The header shows basic details like your name, policy number, and claim number. The service date tells you when you received care. The provider section lists your doctor or treatment facility.

The charges section breaks down costs into clear categories. "Amount charged" shows what your provider billed. "Plan discount" reveals negotiated rate reductions. "Covered amount" indicates what your insurance actually pays. "Your responsibility" shows what you owe.[3]

Most EOBs include a summary of benefits used. This tracks your progress toward annual limits like deductibles and out-of-pocket maximums. For conditions like anxiety disorders or bipolar disorder requiring ongoing care, this tracking becomes essential for budget planning.

The explanation codes section uses numbers or letters to explain coverage decisions. Code "01" might mean "covered under your plan." Code "97" could indicate "service not covered." These codes vary by insurer, but most EOBs include a key explaining what each code means.

Look for the claim status indicator. This shows whether the claim was "paid," "denied," or "pending." Pending claims need follow-up. Denied claims require careful review to understand why coverage was refused.

Treatment-Specific Codes and Terms

Behavioral health treatment uses specific medical codes that appear on your EOB. Procedure codes start with numbers like 90791 for psychiatric diagnostic evaluation or 90834 for individual therapy sessions. Understanding these codes helps you verify you received the services billed.[4]

Diagnosis codes begin with letters and numbers like F41.1 for generalized anxiety disorder or F33.1 for major depression. These codes must match your actual diagnosis. Incorrect diagnosis codes can lead to claim denials or coverage problems later.

Different treatment settings use different codes. Outpatient therapy uses one set of codes. Intensive outpatient programs use others. Residential treatment facilities have their own coding system. Understanding these differences helps you spot billing errors.

Medication-related codes appear if you receive psychiatric medications during treatment. These might include administration codes or monitoring codes. For conditions like ADHD requiring medication management, these codes become frequent on EOBs.

Some EOBs combine multiple services into single line items. A treatment session might include therapy and medication management. Ask your provider to clarify combined services if the EOB seems unclear. You have the right to understand exactly what services you received and how they were billed.

Understanding Your Costs

EOBs break down your financial responsibility into several categories. Your deductible is the amount you pay before insurance coverage begins. Copayments are fixed amounts you pay per visit. Coinsurance is a percentage you pay after meeting your deductible.[5]

Out-of-network costs appear higher on EOBs because you receive less coverage. If you see an out-of-network provider for eating disorders treatment, your EOB will show higher patient responsibility amounts. The "not covered" section shows services your plan excludes entirely.

Some EOBs show "patient refund due" if you overpaid at the time of service. This happens when your provider collects more than your actual responsibility. Contact your provider about refunds shown on your EOB. They should process refunds within a reasonable timeframe.

Annual out-of-pocket maximums protect you from excessive costs. Your EOB tracks progress toward this limit. Once you reach the maximum, your insurance pays 100% of covered services for the rest of the year. This protection becomes especially valuable for intensive treatments.

Prior authorization requirements appear on EOBs when services need advance approval. If you received care without required authorization, your EOB might show reduced coverage or full denial. Understanding authorization requirements prevents surprise costs.

When Claims Are Denied

Denied claims show specific reasons on your EOB. Common denial reasons include "not medically necessary," "experimental treatment," or "provider not in network." Each denial reason has specific appeal rights and timeframes.[6]

"Not medically necessary" denials require clinical documentation from your provider. Your doctor must prove the treatment meets medical standards for your condition. This becomes important for conditions like co-occurring disorders where complex treatment approaches may face scrutiny.

Prior authorization denials indicate required approvals were not obtained. Sometimes providers submit authorization requests that get denied or delayed. Check with your provider about authorization status before assuming coverage problems.

Partial denials cover some services but deny others from the same treatment episode. Your provider might bill for group therapy and individual therapy, but insurance only covers individual sessions. Understanding partial denials helps you advocate for complete coverage.

The EOB includes appeal deadlines, usually 60 to 180 days from the denial date. Missing these deadlines can eliminate your right to challenge the decision. Mark appeal deadlines on your calendar immediately after receiving a denied claim EOB.

Some denials result from simple billing errors. Incorrect codes, wrong dates of service, or missing information can trigger denials. Your provider can resubmit corrected claims to resolve these technical problems without formal appeals.

Spotting Parity Violations

The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act requires insurers to provide equal coverage for mental health and medical care. EOBs can reveal parity violations through unequal treatment of behavioral health claims.[7]

Compare your mental health EOBs to medical care EOBs. If you face higher copays, stricter prior authorization requirements, or more frequent denials for behavioral health services, you may be experiencing parity violations. Document these differences for potential complaints.

Annual and lifetime limits on mental health services violate parity laws if medical services have no similar limits. Your EOB might show "benefits exhausted" for therapy sessions while medical visits remain covered. This represents a clear parity violation.

Prior authorization requirements must be comparable between mental health and medical services. If your insurer requires authorization for every therapy session but not for similar medical treatments, this creates unequal access that violates parity principles.

Network adequacy issues appear on EOBs when you cannot find in-network providers for conditions like alcohol use disorder treatment. If you consistently receive out-of-network coverage because of provider shortages, your insurer may be violating network adequacy requirements.

Keep detailed records of suspected parity violations. Compare similar services over time. Document provider availability issues. This information becomes crucial for filing complaints with state insurance departments or federal agencies.

Next Steps After Reviewing Your EOB

Contact your provider's billing department if you find errors on your EOB. Common errors include wrong diagnosis codes, incorrect service dates, or duplicate charges. Providers can resubmit corrected claims to resolve billing mistakes.

File formal appeals for denied claims you believe should be covered. Include supporting documentation from your provider. Explain how the denied services relate to your treatment plan. Appeal letters should be clear, factual, and submitted within required deadlines.[8]

Request detailed explanation of coverage decisions from your insurer. You have the right to understand the specific criteria used to evaluate your claims. This information helps you and your provider better navigate coverage requirements for ongoing treatment.

Consider external appeals for upheld denials. Independent reviewers evaluate your case when internal appeals fail. External appeals are free to consumers and provide binding decisions on coverage disputes. Success rates for external appeals reach 40-50% across different types of care.

Document patterns of coverage problems for regulatory complaints. State insurance departments investigate systematic parity violations or unfair claim practices. Federal agencies also accept complaints about employer-sponsored plans under ERISA (Employee Retirement Income Security Act).

Track your healthcare spending using EOB information. This documentation becomes valuable for tax deductions, flexible spending account claims, and budget planning. Maintain organized files of all EOBs for future reference and potential audits.

Communicate with your treatment provider about coverage patterns you observe. Providers can adjust treatment approaches, timing, or billing practices to optimize your insurance benefits while maintaining effective care standards for your condition.

Clinical Significance: Understanding EOBs empowers patients to advocate for proper coverage and identify violations of mental health parity laws. This knowledge helps ensure access to necessary behavioral health treatment while avoiding unexpected financial burdens.

Reading EOBs for Adolescent Behavioral Health Treatment

When a teenager receives behavioral health treatment, parents will receive Explanation of Benefits statements for each service billed. Understanding these EOBs is essential for verifying that the insurer is applying mental health parity correctly and that claims are being processed accurately.

Adolescent behavioral health treatment often involves multiple concurrent services — individual therapy, family therapy, group therapy, psychiatric evaluation, and medication management — that may be billed separately. Each service generates its own EOB line item. Parents reviewing EOBs for a teen in intensive outpatient treatment or residential treatment may receive complex multi-page documents covering dozens of discrete service codes.

Key things to verify on an EOB for adolescent treatment: that the services billed match the services actually received, that the cost-sharing applied (copay, deductible, coinsurance) matches what your plan documents specify for mental health services, and that the insurer has not applied visit limits or prior authorization denials that would not apply to equivalent physical health services under mental health parity law.

If an EOB shows a claim denied or applied at a higher cost-sharing tier than expected, contact your insurer immediately and request the specific reason code and coverage criteria used. Denials for adolescent residential or intensive outpatient treatment are frequently appealable on both medical necessity and parity grounds. Filing a formal appeal is the appropriate next step when an EOB reveals unexpected cost exposure.
